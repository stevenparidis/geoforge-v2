Master Geological Reference

Plain-English → 3D Annotated Model

A comprehensive learning and specification document covering structural geology, lithology, stratigraphy, mineralisation, and the vocabulary needed to interpret natural-language site descriptions into a 3D geological model.



How to use this document

This is a single, comprehensive reference for the GeoForge project. It serves two purposes:


As a learning tool — an experienced geology student can read through it to understand structural geology, stratigraphy, igneous and metamorphic processes, and the mineralisation styles that flow from them.


As a feedable specification — every section provides the structured data that an AI interpreter needs to convert plain-English site descriptions into the GeoForge JSON schema and the 3D annotated model.

Each formation entry includes: a clear definition, the stress regime or process that drives it, the geological factors that influence it, the exact label fields the 3D model should render, and the plain-English vocabulary that should trigger it in the AI interpreter.


Convention used throughout: σ1 = maximum principal stress, σ2 = intermediate, σ3 = minimum. Dip is the angle a plane makes with horizontal (0-90°). Strike is the compass bearing of a horizontal line on that plane (0-360°).

1. Fundamentals — stress, strain, deformation

Every geological structure is a record of how rock responded to stress. Understanding these terms is essential because they are what the AI interpreter must read from plain English and convert into structural events in the model.


Principal stress axes


σ1 — maximum principal stress, the strongest compressive direction.


σ2 — intermediate principal stress, perpendicular to both σ1 and σ3.


σ3 — minimum principal stress; tensile fractures open perpendicular to this.


Anderson's fault classification

Anderson's theory links the orientation of σ1 to the type of fault produced. This is the bridge between stress and structure that the AI must understand.

Brittle vs ductile deformation


Brittle — rock fractures without flowing. Dominates at shallow depths (typically <10–15 km), low temperatures, fast strain rates. Produces faults and joints.


Ductile — rock flows like very stiff putty. Dominates at depth, high temperatures, slow strain rates. Produces folds and shear zones.


Brittle-ductile transition — the depth at which behaviour shifts; varies by lithology and geothermal gradient, but typically around 300–400 °C.


Strain markers


Elongation — lengthening of a line.


Shortening % — how much a unit has been compressed.


Finite strain ellipsoid — the shape that records total accumulated strain in three dimensions; axes are X (longest), Y (intermediate), Z (shortest).


Pure shear — coaxial strain; no rotation.


Simple shear — non-coaxial strain; produces rotation, typical of shear zones.


Rheology controls

Rheology is how a rock flows. The same stress produces different structures depending on these controls:


Temperature — higher = more ductile.


Pressure (confining) — higher = more ductile, suppresses fracturing.


Strain rate — slower = more ductile.


Mineralogy — quartz weakens at lower temp than feldspar; mica is very weak.


Fluid pressure — high pore fluid pressure dramatically reduces effective stress and promotes faulting.


Grain size — finer grain = more ductile in many regimes.


Byerlee's Law: friction on faults is roughly constant (coefficient ~0.6–0.85) across most rocks, meaning faults reactivate at predictable shear-to-normal stress ratios regardless of rock type.

3. Stratigraphy and layering

The base of every GeoForge model is a stack of layers. These are the fundamental building blocks before any structural events are applied.


Stratigraphic principles


Law of superposition — in an undisturbed sequence, older rocks lie below younger rocks.


Original horizontality — sediments are deposited in horizontal layers; any tilt is from later tectonics.


Lateral continuity — layers extend laterally until they thin, pinch out, or are eroded.


Cross-cutting relationships — anything that cuts across a feature must be younger than that feature.


Inclusions — a clast inside a rock is older than the rock containing it.

6. Folds

Folds form when rock layers bend rather than break. They are produced primarily by compressional stress in the ductile regime, but can also form in extensional or shear settings depending on geometry.


Fold anatomy — terms every fold annotation needs


Hinge — line of maximum curvature.


Axial plane — surface that bisects the angle between the two limbs.


Limbs — the two sides of the fold.


Interlimb angle — the angle between the two limbs; defines fold tightness.


Plunge — angle the hinge line makes with horizontal.


Vergence — direction the fold is overturned; indicates transport direction.


Wavelength — horizontal distance between successive hinges of the same type.


Amplitude — vertical height from inflection line to hinge.

7. Faults

Faults are brittle fractures with measurable displacement. They are the most important structures for both basin formation and mineralisation.


Fault anatomy


Fault plane — the surface along which movement occurs.


Hanging wall — the block sitting above the fault plane.


Footwall — the block sitting below the fault plane.


Strike — compass bearing of a horizontal line on the fault plane.


Dip — angle the fault plane makes with horizontal.


Throw — the vertical component of displacement.


Heave — the horizontal component of displacement.


Net slip — the true distance and direction of movement on the fault plane.


Rake (pitch) — angle between slip direction and the strike, measured on the fault plane.


Fault zone components


Fault core — the thin slip surface itself, often containing fault gouge.


Fault gouge — fine clay-rich crushed rock from grinding; often impermeable.


Fault breccia — angular fragments of rock cemented together; common ore host.


Damage zone — fractured rock around the core; permeable and fluid-bearing.


Slickenlines / slickensides — polished striated surfaces recording exact slip direction.

Slickenlines and kinematic indicators

These are the small-scale features that record exact movement direction on a fault. The 3D model should annotate them with slip vectors:


Slickenfibres — mineral fibres grown in the slip direction.


Riedel shears — small fractures at 15–30° to main fault, indicate sense of shear.


Drag folds — bent layers near the fault, vergence indicates slip direction.


Offset markers — any pre-existing feature cut by the fault; the offset gives both direction and magnitude.

11. Metamorphic features

Metamorphic features record the temperature, pressure, and strain that rocks experience after burial or near intrusions.


Metamorphic types


Regional metamorphism — large areas affected by burial during orogeny. Produces foliated rocks (slate-phyllite-schist-gneiss sequence with grade).


Contact (thermal) metamorphism — heating near an intrusion. Produces non-foliated hornfels and skarn.


Dynamic (cataclastic) metamorphism — mechanical grinding in fault zones. Produces fault breccia, gouge, and mylonite.


Burial metamorphism — low-grade alteration in deep sedimentary basins.


Hydrothermal metamorphism — alteration by hot fluids; produces alteration halos around deposits.


Impact metamorphism — shock from meteorite impact; produces shatter cones, suevite.

13. Fluid pathways and hydrogeology

Fluids carry metals. Understanding how fluids move through rock is the single biggest predictor of where ore forms. This section is critical for the mineralisation-prediction goal of the learning tool.


Fluid pathway terminology


Aquifer — permeable unit that transmits fluid (sandstone, fractured limestone).


Aquitard / aquiclude — impermeable unit that blocks fluid flow (shale, fault gouge, evaporite).


Hydraulic gradient — direction fluid flows; from high head to low head.


Pore fluid pressure — pressure in pore spaces; high values reduce effective stress and trigger faulting.


Lithostatic pressure — pressure from overlying rock weight; about 27 MPa per km.


Hydrostatic pressure — pressure from a column of water; about 10 MPa per km.


Overpressure — pore fluid pressure exceeding hydrostatic; common in compacting sediments.


Hydrothermal system anatomy

Every hydrothermal ore deposit needs five elements. The 3D model can label these as nodes in a flow diagram on the model:


1. Heat source — magma, deep crust, or geothermal gradient.


2. Fluid source — magmatic, meteoric, basinal brine, or metamorphic.


3. Metal source — country rock, magma itself, or pre-existing deposits.


4. Pathway — faults, fractures, permeable units; the structural plumbing.


5. Trap / depositional site — where physical or chemical conditions cause metal to precipitate.

Detailed deposit type catalogue


Porphyry Cu-Mo-Au

Disseminated and stockwork Cu/Mo/Au mineralisation in and around a felsic to intermediate stock.


Structural control: Radial and concentric fracture systems around an intrusion. Often emplaced at shallow depths (1–5 km) in subduction-related arcs.


Alteration: Concentric zonation: potassic (core), phyllic, argillic, propylitic (outer).


Grade: 0.3–1.5 % Cu, 0.005–0.05 % Mo, 0.1–1 g/t Au.


Examples: Bingham (Utah), El Teniente (Chile), Grasberg (Indonesia), Cadia (Australia).


3D annotation: Stock outline, alteration shells (each as a coloured zone), grade contours, ore shell, depth markers.


Orogenic gold (lode gold)

Gold in quartz-carbonate veins within shear zones in metamorphic terranes.


Structural control: Second-order shear zones off major crustal-scale structures, dilational jogs, fold hinges. Greenschist to amphibolite facies.


Alteration: Carbonate, sericite, sulphide alteration envelope around veins.


Grade: Typically 3–15 g/t Au, can be much higher in shoots.


Examples: Kalgoorlie (Australia), Timmins (Canada), Ashanti (Ghana), Witwatersrand reefs are debated.


3D annotation: Shear zone trace and width, vein sets (strike/dip), ore shoot plunge and direction, host lithology.


VMS (volcanogenic massive sulphide)

Stratiform massive sulphide lenses formed at submarine hydrothermal vents.


Structural control: Syn-volcanic extensional faults, calderas in submarine arcs. Lenses sit at contacts between volcanic and sedimentary units.


Alteration: Footwall stringer zone (alteration pipe) leading up to the massive sulphide lens.


Grade: Polymetallic: 2–15 % Zn, 0.5–6 % Cu, 0.5–4 % Pb, 30–200 g/t Ag.


Examples: Kuroko (Japan), Bathurst (Canada), Iberian Pyrite Belt.


3D annotation: Massive sulphide lens shape, footwall stringer alteration pipe, syn-volcanic fault, host volcanics.


SEDEX (sedimentary exhalative)

Stratiform Zn-Pb-Ag mineralisation in fine-grained sediments, formed by hot brines venting onto the seafloor.


Structural control: Growth faults along rifted basin margins, sub-basin depocentres.


Alteration: Often subtle; silicification, chloritisation in feeder zones.


Grade: 5–20 % combined Zn+Pb, 30–200 g/t Ag.


Examples: Broken Hill (Australia), Mount Isa, Red Dog (Alaska), Sullivan (Canada).


3D annotation: Stratiform ore horizon, bounding growth fault, basin geometry, feeder zone.


MVT (Mississippi Valley type)

Stratabound Zn-Pb in carbonate platforms, no direct igneous link.


Structural control: Basinal brines migrating through carbonate aquifers, focused at faults, karst features, and unconformities.


Alteration: Dolomitisation halo around ore.


Grade: 5–10 % combined Zn+Pb.


Examples: Mississippi Valley district (USA), Pine Point (Canada), Lennard Shelf (Australia).


3D annotation: Host carbonate, dolomitised halo, fault pathway, karst cavities, unconformity surface.


Skarn deposit

Calc-silicate metasomatic deposit at contact of intrusion with carbonate rocks. Can host Cu, Fe, Au, W, Sn, Zn.


Structural control: Intrusion-carbonate contact geometry; faults and bedding planes as fluid pathways.


Alteration: Garnet-pyroxene-magnetite assemblage; prograde to retrograde zonation.


Grade: Varies by metal; 1–5 % Cu typical for Cu skarn.


Examples: Antamina (Peru, Cu-Zn), Bingham Cu skarn margins, MacTung (Canada, W).


3D annotation: Intrusion contact, exoskarn vs endoskarn, calc-silicate zones, ore zone, host carbonate.


Epithermal Au-Ag

Shallow (<1.5 km), low-temperature (<300 °C) vein and breccia gold-silver deposits in volcanic terranes.


Structural control: Normal faults, caldera ring faults, breccia pipes. Boiling level is critical.


Alteration: Two main styles. High-sulphidation (HS): advanced argillic, vuggy silica. Low-sulphidation (LS): adularia-sericite, chalcedony.


Grade: 1–30 g/t Au, often with high Ag.


Examples: Yanacocha (Peru, HS), Hishikari (Japan, LS), Ladolam (PNG).


3D annotation: Vein system, boiling level, alteration halo (HS or LS), host volcanic, ore shoot plunge.


IOCG (iron oxide-copper-gold)

Cu-Au with abundant Fe oxides (magnetite-hematite), sometimes with U, REE, Ag.


Structural control: Deep crustal fault corridors, transpressional to extensional regimes. Often associated with anorogenic granites.


Alteration: Sodic-calcic at depth, potassic-hematite at higher levels.


Grade: 1–2 % Cu, 0.5–1 g/t Au.


Examples: Olympic Dam (Australia), Candelaria (Chile), Salobo (Brazil).


3D annotation: Breccia complex, Fe oxide zones (magnetite vs hematite), fault corridor, alteration zones.


Layered mafic intrusion (PGE, Cr, Ni)

Magmatic stratiform deposits in layered ultramafic-mafic intrusions.


Structural control: Stratigraphic position within the intrusion. Specific PGE 'reefs' or chromitite layers.


Alteration: Generally limited; primary magmatic textures preserved.


Grade: PGE: 3–10 g/t. Cr: 30–45 % Cr2O3 in chromitite layers.


Examples: Bushveld Complex (South Africa), Stillwater (USA), Great Dyke (Zimbabwe).


3D annotation: Cumulate layers, reef position, chromitite layers, intrusion contact.


Ni-Cu sulphide (magmatic)

Ni-Cu-PGE sulphide accumulations at the base of mafic intrusions or in feeder conduits.


Structural control: Footwall embayments, feeder dykes, conduit constrictions where sulphide settles.


Alteration: Limited magmatic; some serpentinisation.


Grade: 0.5–3 % Ni, 0.5–2 % Cu, modest PGE.


Examples: Sudbury (Canada), Norilsk (Russia), Voisey's Bay (Canada).


3D annotation: Conduit geometry, basal sulphide layer, host intrusion, country rock contact.


Kimberlite-hosted diamond

Diamonds carried from mantle in kimberlite or lamproite pipes.


Structural control: Vertical pipes, deep cratonic settings, often along structural weaknesses in lithosphere.


Alteration: Serpentinisation of olivine; carbonate alteration.


Grade: Carats per hundred tonnes; varies hugely.


Examples: Kimberley (South Africa), Argyle (Australia, lamproite), Diavik (Canada).


3D annotation: Pipe outline, crater facies, diatreme facies, root zone, host craton.


Uranium roll-front

Crescent-shaped U deposits in permeable sandstones at redox boundaries.


Structural control: Permeable sandstone aquifer, gentle dip, oxidising-to-reducing transition.


Alteration: Oxidised tongue, reduced front, ore at the boundary.


Grade: 0.05–0.3 % U3O8.


Examples: Wyoming Powder River, Kazakhstan deposits.


3D annotation: Permeable host, redox interface, ore roll, fluid flow direction.


Bauxite (Al)

Residual deposits of Al hydroxide minerals from extreme tropical weathering.


Structural control: Stable cratonic plateaus, tropical climate, time. No structural control beyond palaeoclimate.


Alteration: Leaching of all soluble elements; residual Al, Fe, Ti.


Grade: 40–60 % Al2O3.


Examples: Weipa (Australia), Jamaica, Guinea.


3D annotation: Laterite profile zones, parent rock, water table position.


Banded iron formation (BIF)

Alternating Fe-rich and Si-rich layers; mostly Precambrian.


Structural control: Stratiform; later structural deformation can enrich and remobilise.


Alteration: Supergene enrichment removes silica, upgrades Fe.


Grade: 30–35 % Fe (primary), 60+ % Fe (supergene enriched).


Examples: Hamersley (Australia), Carajás (Brazil), Lake Superior (Canada/USA).


3D annotation: Banded stratigraphy, weathering profile, enrichment zone, basin context.


Placer (alluvial)

Mechanical concentration of dense or hard minerals in stream beds, beaches, or palaeochannels.


Structural control: Drainage geometry, gradient changes, traps such as pools and inside bends.


Alteration: None; primary minerals.


Grade: Variable; from sub-gram Au to high-grade gemstone deposits.


Examples: Witwatersrand (palaeoplacer, debated), California, Yukon, beach REE sands.


3D annotation: Channel geometry, palaeocurrent direction, gravel layer, bedrock topography.

17. 3D annotation specification

The annotation system is the visual contract between the model and the student. It is how the model teaches. Every annotation should answer: what is this, and why is it here?


Annotation rules


Labels are always toggleable via a 'Show labels' button — clean view by default, full-annotation view on demand.


Labels billboard toward the camera so they remain readable from any angle.


Labels use leader lines (thin grey lines) from the label text to the feature.


If two labels would overlap in the current view, the higher-priority label wins; the other fades or moves.


Priority order: faults > unconformities > folds > major lithology contacts > minor features > rock type labels > grade contours.


Numeric values use SI units by default (metres, degrees), with US units available as a setting.


Each annotation should be clickable — clicking shows the original plain-English sentence that produced it.
